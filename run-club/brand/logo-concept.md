# Mozaka Run Club — Logo Brief

---

## Primary Logo Concept

**The mark: The Striding Sun**

A circle — precisely drawn, not hand-cut — bisected by a single diagonal line at 40°. The circle reads simultaneously as: a rising sun cresting the horizon; a chess piece (the pawn's silhouette) seen from the front; a vinyl record (a nod to the DJ set that follows the run); the "O" in MOZAKA. The diagonal is the stride — a running leg caught mid-push, or the angular path a chess knight takes across a board.

The relationship between circle and diagonal is the mark's entire meaning: the moment of crossing. The sun crossing the horizon. The runner crossing the finish line. The stranger crossing into a friendship.

**The wordmark: "MOZAKA RUN CLUB"**

Set in Big Shoulders Display 900 (ultra-condensed, all-caps). Three stacked words in a tight flush-left block:

```
MOZAKA
RUN
CLUB
```

The three-word stack is intentional — it echoes the brand's three-word tagline (Run. Dance. Chess.) and creates a tight rectangular block that the mark sits beside in the primary lockup. The "O" in MOZAKA optionally carries the same circle-bisect motif at small sizes — or is left clean for legibility.

**Mark and wordmark relationship:**
The mark sits to the left of the wordmark at the height of the full three-line block. Clear space between mark and wordmark equals the width of the vertical line in the circle. On horizontal lockups, they sit on a common baseline defined by the bottom of "CLUB." On vertical lockups (event banners, posts), the mark sits above the wordmark, centered.

**What it evokes:**
Precision with warmth. The circle is never wobbly — this isn't the hand-cut worldmark energy of the main Mozaka brand. It is technically drawn, confident, and exact. But the diagonal's angle is specific (not 45°, not 30° — 40°, the angle of a runner's push-phase stride) giving it life without randomness. It says: *someone thought about this.*

## Secondary / Icon Version

**The Solo Mark:** The circle-and-diagonal alone, no wordmark. This is the sticker, the wristband stamp, the Instagram avatar, the embossed detail on the socks, the app shortcut icon.

At 32px, the diagonal becomes a simple dividing line — the circle remains. At 16px, it reduces to a divided circle — the yin-yang of running and rest. At 512px (large digital), the slight weight difference between the circle stroke and the diagonal stroke becomes visible and intentional — heavier circle (permanence) lighter diagonal (motion).

**The Monogram — "MRC":**
Set in Big Shoulders Display 900, tracked to 0, the three letters M-R-C stacked or set in an equilateral triangle arrangement. Used for: embroidery on vest, watermark on photography, small print on packaging tape.

## Logo Don'ts

1. **Never hand-cut or irregularize the mark.** The main Mozaka brand owns the hand-cut aesthetic. The Run Club mark is technically precise — that distinction is how the two identities coexist in the same universe without collision.
2. **Never put the circle-diagonal in a box, shield, or badge.** The mark IS the container. Adding a container is redundant and suburban.
3. **Never add a drop shadow, gradient, or glow to the mark.** The mark reads through contrast alone. If it doesn't read on a surface, the surface is wrong — not the mark.
4. **Never set the wordmark in anything other than Big Shoulders Display 900.** Other weights collapse the geometry. Other typefaces are a different brand.
5. **Never rotate the full lockup.** The diagonal already carries the motion. Rotating the lockup makes the composition fight itself. The only exception: the mark alone can tilt ±3° on a wristband wrap.

## Color Variations

**On light background (morning run — Pumzi cream or white)**
Mark in Dawu orange. Wordmark in Usiku (deep indigo). This is the default morning palette — warm and energetic against the brightness of a 7am post.

**On dark background (after party / event poster — Usiku)**
Mark in Volt (electric chartreuse). Wordmark in Pumzi (cream). The volt mark cuts through photography and dark fields the way a headlamp cuts through pre-dawn dark — technical, earned, unmistakable.

**On a running vest (performance fabric)**
Mark in Volt embroidered or heat-transferred. Wordmark in Pumzi. Single-color transfer preferred for cost and legibility at speed. The vest back carries the full lockup; the front carries the solo mark at the heart position.

**On a coffee cup / merch**
Two options: (1) Single Dawu orange mark + wordmark on Pumzi/cream card stock. (2) Full Usiku (dark) cup with the solo mark in Volt at 30% cup height — the logo as the only thing on the cup. No taglines, no hashtags. The object speaks for the brand.

## Inspiration References

**1. Nike Swoosh (by Carolyn Davidson, 1971)**
*Why:* Not because it's famous — but because it is the purest case study in a mark that contains motion without depicting it literally. The swoosh doesn't show a running shoe. It implies momentum through shape alone. The Mozaka Run Club mark should aim for the same economy: the circle-diagonal doesn't show a runner, it implies the crossing moment. The lesson: restraint in the mark forces all the personality into how you use it.

**2. Stüssy wordmark**
*Why:* Not for its visual form, but for its relationship to the main parent brand. Stüssy has a dozen sub-lines and collaborations that each carry their own graphic identity — but you always know they share a lineage, because they're made by the same sensibility. The Run Club mark should feel like it was made by the same hand that drew the Mozaka helmet crest — same design discipline, different gesture. Stüssy proves you can run multiple identities from one brand voice.

**3. Boiler Room logo mark**
*Why:* Boiler Room is a brand that lives in event photography, dark backgrounds, and chaotic physical environments — and their mark (a simple bold wordmark, sometimes reduced to "BR") survives all of it because it has almost no visual noise. The Mozaka Run Club mark should share this resilience: it should read on a blurry finish line photo, on a low-quality wristband print, on a scratched coffee cup. The test: can you see it from 5 metres, in bad light, at a slight angle? If yes, it's working.
